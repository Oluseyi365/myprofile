About

Bridging the clinic
and the laboratory

I am a physician-scientist and doctoral researcher at The Catholic University of America, working in Dr. Pamela L. Tuma's laboratory. My research sits at the intersection of cell biology, metabolism, and liver disease — investigating how alcohol rewires protein acetylation to disrupt the hepatocyte's internal trafficking machinery.

My clinical training as a physician (MBChB) informs every experiment I design. I bring a translational lens to mechanistic questions, always asking: what does this mean for a patient? What does it mean for a drug target?

Long-term, I am building toward a career as a translational physician-scientist in biotechnology — where I can drive therapeutic development from first principles, informed by both patient need and molecular insight.

Research Focus

Decoding the
acetylated hepatocyte

01
Ethanol & Protein Acetylation

Characterizing how ethanol exposure alters the hepatic acetylome and how these post-translational modifications impair intracellular vesicular trafficking and organelle homeostasis in WIF-B cells.

Acetyl-CoA WIF-B Cells Hepatic Trafficking
02
ACLY Inhibition as Therapeutic Strategy

Translating clinical pharmacokinetics of bempedoic acid into in vitro dosing models. Comparing bempedoic acid and hydroxycitrate as ACLY inhibitors to modulate acetyl-CoA availability and restore hepatic function.

Bempedoic Acid Hydroxycitrate ACLY
03
Autophagy & Organelle Dynamics

Quantifying autophagy flux markers including p62 and LC3 puncta via immunofluorescence imaging to understand how caloric restriction mimetics rescue autophagic degradation in alcohol-exposed hepatocytes.

Autophagy LC3 / p62 Immunofluorescence
